# Oxidation Rates

## How Much Can Your Body Actually Use?

Exogenous carbohydrate oxidation -- the rate at which your body can burn ingested carbs during exercise -- has a well-documented ceiling that depends entirely on intestinal absorption capacity.

## The Numbers

| Carbohydrate Source | Max Oxidation Rate |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Glucose only | ~60 g/h (1.0 g/min) |
| Maltodextrin only | ~60 g/h (1.0 g/min) |
| Glucose + Fructose (2:1) | ~90 g/h (1.5 g/min) |
| Peak demonstrated in studies | ~105 g/h (1.75 g/min) |
| Modern ultra-athletes (trained gut) | 100-120 g/h |

## The Dose-Response Relationship

Smith et al. (2013) conducted a landmark multicenter study with 51 cyclists across 4 research sites, testing 12 different carbohydrate doses from 10-120 g/h. Key findings:

- Performance improvement is **dose-dependent** up to a point
- Greatest enhancement occurred at **60-80 g/h** for glucose:fructose mixtures during ~3h events
- Beyond ~90 g/h, returns diminish for most athletes without specific gut training

## Performance Impact

Currell & Jeukendrup (2008) demonstrated the real-world effect:
- Glucose + Fructose improved cycling time trial power by **8%** over glucose alone (275 vs. 254 W)
- Both carbohydrate conditions already outperformed placebo significantly

## Important Caveats

- **Body weight independent:** A 60 kg runner and a 90 kg cyclist need the same intake rates. The gut, not the muscles, is the bottleneck.
- **Training status independent:** Trained and untrained athletes show similar exogenous oxidation rates at matched intensity.
- **Intensity matters:** Below ~50% VO2max, exogenous oxidation is reduced (lower metabolic demand).
- **Individual variation:** Gut training, genetics, and habitual diet all influence personal limits.

## Key References

- Jeukendrup & Jentjens (2000). *Sports Med* -- Established the ~1 g/min ceiling (>40 studies)
- Jentjens et al. (2004). *J Appl Physiol* -- Breakthrough: 1.26 g/min with glucose + fructose
- Currell & Jeukendrup (2008). *Med Sci Sports Exerc* -- 8% performance improvement
- Smith et al. (2013). *Med Sci Sports Exerc* -- Multicenter dose-response study
- Rowlands et al. (2012). *Med Sci Sports Exerc* -- Maltodextrin:fructose reduced MTB race time by 1.8%
