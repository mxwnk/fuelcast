# Gut Tolerance and Training

## Your Gut Is Trainable

Gastrointestinal (GI) distress is the most common reason athletes fail to meet their carbohydrate targets during competition. The good news: the gut adapts to increased carbohydrate intake through a genuine physiological process.

## The Science of Gut Training

Cox et al. (2010) demonstrated that a **28-day high-carbohydrate protocol** (6.5 g/kg/day with carbohydrate supplementation during training sessions) significantly increased exogenous carbohydrate oxidation rates compared to a control diet.

The mechanism: intestinal SGLT1 and GLUT5 transporters are **upregulated** in response to increased dietary carbohydrate load. More transporters = more absorption capacity = less unabsorbed carbohydrate causing GI issues.

### Practical Gut Training Protocol

1. Start 4-6 weeks before your target race
2. Begin at a comfortable intake rate (e.g., 40-50 g/h)
3. Progressively increase by 10 g/h each week
4. Use the same products and concentrations you plan to race with
5. Practice during training sessions at race-relevant intensity

## Common Causes of GI Distress

| Cause | Mechanism | Solution |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| High concentration (>8%) | Slows gastric emptying, osmotic fluid draw into gut | Dilute to 6-8% or use gels + water |
| Excess fructose | Unabsorbed fructose is osmotically active in colon | Respect 2:1 ratio, increase gradually |
| Dehydration (>2% BW loss) | Reduced splanchnic blood flow | Maintain hydration plan |
| High intensity (>80% VO2max) | Blood diverted from gut to muscles | Reduce intake at peak efforts |
| Fiber/fat/protein pre-race | Slows gastric emptying, increases residue | Low-fiber, low-fat meals before racing |
| NSAIDs (ibuprofen, etc.) | Damage gut lining, increase permeability | Avoid during exercise |
| No prior practice | Untrained gut has fewer transporters | Gut training protocol |

## Osmolality: Why It Matters

- **Isotonic range:** 280-320 mOsm/kg (matched to blood plasma)
- **Hypertonic solutions (>320 mOsm/kg):** Draw water INTO the gut lumen, causing bloating and diarrhea
- **Maltodextrin advantage:** A 60 g/L maltodextrin solution has far lower osmolality than a 60 g/L glucose solution because fewer individual molecules are in solution (large polymers vs. small monomers)

**This is why maltodextrin is preferred over glucose powder for DIY mixes** -- you can achieve high carbohydrate concentrations while maintaining near-isotonic osmolality.

## Heat and Hydration Interaction

- Hot conditions increase GI issues due to splanchnic hypoperfusion
- Dehydration compounds the problem (less blood available for the gut)
- Athletes should **reduce planned carbohydrate concentration** in extreme heat
- Cooler fluids (10-15 degrees C) may offer marginal gastric emptying benefits

## Key Statistics

- **30-50%** of endurance athletes report GI symptoms during competition (de Oliveira et al., 2014)
- Multiple transportable carbohydrates (maltodextrin:fructose) **reduced abdominal cramps by 8.1 points** on a 0-100 scale vs. glucose alone (Rowlands et al., 2012)
- Pfeiffer et al. (2012) found that in Ironman athletes, higher CHO intake correlated with better performance, but GI issues were more common with concentrated solutions
