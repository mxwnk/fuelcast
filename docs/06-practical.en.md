# Practical Application

## From Science to Race Day

Understanding the theory is one thing -- applying it in practice is where performance gains are realized. Here is how the science translates to actionable fueling strategies.

## Gels vs. Drinks vs. Real Food

Pfeiffer et al. (2010) demonstrated that **all formats achieve similar oxidation rates** when carbohydrate type and total amount are matched:

| Format | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| Gels | Precise dosing, portable, fast | Need separate water, concentrated | High-intensity racing |
| Drinks | Hydration + fuel combined, easy to sip | Heavy to carry, concentration fixed | Cycling (bottle cages) |
| Solids (bars, chews) | Higher satiety, variety | Slower gastric emptying, harder at intensity | Ultra-events, moderate pace |

**Key insight:** The delivery format is a practical choice, not a physiological one. Pick what you can consistently consume at race intensity.

## DIY Mixing: The Maltodextrin + Fructose Approach

### Why Maltodextrin?

| Property | Maltodextrin | Glucose Powder |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Molecular weight | 900-6000 Da | 180 Da |
| Osmolality (100g/L) | ~100-150 mOsm/kg | ~550+ mOsm/kg |
| Sweetness | Very low (neutral taste) | High |
| Transporter | SGLT1 (same as glucose) | SGLT1 |
| Digestion speed | Very fast (already nearly glucose) | Immediate |

Maltodextrin allows **high-concentration bottles that still empty quickly from the stomach** because fewer molecules are in solution. This is the fundamental advantage for DIY sports drinks.

### Example Recipe (per 750 ml bottle, targeting 90 g/h)

| Ingredient | Amount | Purpose |
|------------|--------|---------|
| Maltodextrin (DE 12-19) | 60 g | Glucose source via SGLT1 |
| Fructose | 30 g | Second transporter (GLUT5) |
| Table salt (NaCl) | 1/4 tsp (~1.5 g) | Sodium for SGLT1 co-transport + electrolyte |
| Water | Fill to 750 ml | Hydration |
| Optional: citric acid | Pinch | Taste/palatability |

**Solution concentration:** 12% -- this is above the "ideal" 6-8% but tolerable because maltodextrin's low osmolality keeps the effective osmolality manageable. This is the same principle commercial carb drinks use.

## Race Day vs. Training Fueling

### Race Day Rules
- Use **maximum tolerated intake** (as trained)
- Never try new products or concentrations
- Start fueling within 15 minutes
- Have backup nutrition (in case of GI issues, reduce concentration)

### Training Fueling
- Use to practice and refine race strategy
- Progressively increase intake over 4-6 weeks
- Some sessions may deliberately train low (reduce carbs for metabolic adaptation)
- The race plan should be rehearsed at least 3-4 times before competition

## The Golden Rules

1. **Train your gut** -- at least 4 weeks before race day
2. **Use multiple transportable carbs** above 60 g/h
3. **Start early** -- within 15-30 minutes
4. **Little and often** -- every 15-20 minutes
5. **6-8% concentration** in bottles (or gels + water)
6. **Nothing new on race day**
